Welcome to The Bookshelf!

Hi, I’m Chris. I love books.

I was going to say I love reading books, but I own far too many that I haven’t yet read, so maybe the first statement is more accurate.

I’ve been keeping track of my reading through various means since 2007. At various times I’ve used my blog, Goodreads, and iOS apps, and never been 100% happy with any of them. I’m sure I won’t be 100% happy with this site, either, but it’s my favorite so far!

Here are a few of the books I’ve read recently:

On the Origin of Time

by Thomas Hertog · published 2023 · read 2023-10-14
★ ★ ★ ★ ★
Hertog studied quantum physics under Stephen Hawking and here overviews Hawking's progression of thought in quantum physics, ending in a remarkable position regaining the importance of the human perspective within these theories. You don't expect to get to the last chapter of a book like this and have it start quoting Hannah Arendt, and yet this one did. Thoughtful, not overly simplified but simplified enough for nerdy laymen like me to be able to wade through it... quite a book.

The Odyssey

by Homer · published 2017 · read 2023-09-29
★ ★ ★ ★ ★
Emily Wilson's translation of The Odyssey pulled me in in a way that other translations have failed to grab me. Obviously an epic story, but her translation choices made it come alive in reading.

Average Jones

by Samuel Adams · published 2022 · read 2023-09-14
★ ★ ★ ☆ ☆
Short detective stories in the vein of Sherlock Holmes, but set in early 20th-century America. They're fine, but mostly serve to remind you just how good Sir Arthur Conan Doyle's stories were by comparison.

Translation State

by Ann Leckie · published 2023 · read 2023-09-05
★ ★ ★ ★ ★
A brilliant examination of gender and personhood wrapped up in an adventure story. Really enjoyed this one.

The Ultimate Quest

by Jordan Haynie Ware · published 2017 · read 2023-09-03
★ ★ ★ ★ ☆
A short handbook intro to the Episcopal Church. The subtitle "A Geek's Guide to The Episcopal Church" tells you where it's going, and it goes HARD. Some of the geek references were helpful; some of them seemed pretty over-the-top, but you can't deny the author's commitment to the bit. Still, it's a book I might hand to my college aged kid if they had any interested in attending.
